WHAT IS A FACTOR?

A factor is any whole number that divides evenly into another number — with no remainder. Every number can be broken apart (factored) into pairs of numbers that multiply together to make it.

Think of it like this: if you're a security agent cracking a code, understanding factors tells you all the different ways a number can be built. The more factors a number has, the more ways it can be split — and the easier it is to find patterns in encrypted data.

PRIME VS. COMPOSITE — THE KEY DIFFERENCE

Notice that 3 and 5 only have exactly 2 factors (1 and themselves). These are prime numbers — they can't be broken down into other whole number pairs.

8, 12, and 24 have more than 2 factors. These are composite numbers — they can be split multiple ways.

And 1? It has only one factor (itself). It's neither prime nor composite — it's its own special category.

WHY DIVISIBILITY RULES MATTER

A divisibility rule is a quick shortcut that lets you check whether a number is a factor of another — without doing long division. For a bank security agent working with large encryption codes, these rules are essential tools for breaking down numbers fast.

2
RULE FOR 2 The last digit is 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8
Ex: 346 → last digit 6 ✓  |  571 → last digit 1 ✗
3
RULE FOR 3 Sum of all digits is a multiple of 3
Ex: 123 → 1+2+3=6, 6÷3=2 ✓  |  124 → 1+2+4=7 ✗
4
RULE FOR 4 Last two digits form a multiple of 4
Ex: 312 → 12÷4=3 ✓  |  318 → 18÷4=4.5 ✗
5
RULE FOR 5 Last digit is 0 or 5
Ex: 340 → last digit 0 ✓  |  343 → last digit 3 ✗
6
RULE FOR 6 Both the rule for 2 AND rule for 3 apply
Ex: 234 → even ✓, 2+3+4=9 ✓ → divisible by 6 ✓
8
RULE FOR 8 Last three digits form a multiple of 8
Ex: 1,024 → 024=24, 24÷8=3 ✓  |  1,022 → 022÷8=2.75 ✗
9
RULE FOR 9 Sum of all digits is a multiple of 9
Ex: 729 → 7+2+9=18, 18÷9=2 ✓  |  728 → sum=17 ✗
10
RULE FOR 10 Last digit is 0
Ex: 450 → last digit 0 ✓  |  455 → last digit 5 ✗
⚠️   1 and 7 — No simple divisibility shortcut exists for these. Every number is divisible by 1, so no test is needed. For 7, the only reliable method is to do the division or use long division. These are the agents that play by their own rules.

WHAT MAKES A NUMBER PRIME?

A prime number has exactly two distinct factors: 1 and itself. It cannot be divided evenly by any other whole number. This makes prime numbers the "atoms" of mathematics — the indivisible building blocks that all other numbers are made from.

A composite number has more than two factors — meaning it can be broken down into smaller whole number pairs. Most numbers you encounter every day are composite. Real bank encryption relies on primes precisely because they resist being broken apart.

And 1 is neither prime nor composite — it's its own special case with only one factor.

🌍 REAL WORLD CONNECTIONS

Whether a number is prime or composite changes how useful it is in the real world. Composite numbers are flexible — they split evenly in many ways. Primes are stubborn — they refuse to split, which makes them unpredictable and powerful for security.

🍳
COOKING
12 cookies (composite) split evenly among 2, 3, 4, or 6 people — great for sharing. 13 cookies (prime)? Someone always gets an extra. Primes don't share well.
🌱
GARDENING
12 seeds (composite) can form a 3×4, 2×6, or 1×12 grid. 17 seeds (prime) can only make a 1×17 row — one long line, no rectangles possible.
⚽
SPORTS
11 players on a soccer team (prime) — can't split into equal sub-groups for drills. 12 players (composite) can drill in pairs, trios, or groups of 4 or 6.
🔐
ENCRYPTION
A composite key has factors an attacker can find quickly. A key built from two large primes multiplied together has no easy shortcut — making it the foundation of real-world bank security.

💡 WHY PRIME NUMBERS MAKE THE BEST KEYS

Here's the secret the security division doesn't usually share with recruits: when you multiply two large primes together, you get a number that looks ordinary. But to figure out which two primes were used, an attacker would have to test thousands — sometimes millions — of possibilities. There's no shortcut.

Your encryption key in this mission won't be large enough to stop a real supercomputer, but you'll understand the exact same principle used to protect real bank accounts, passwords, and government communications around the world. That's what makes this math matter.
